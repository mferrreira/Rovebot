# STYLEGUIDE.md

## Scope and confidence

This guide is derived from the writing in this conversation, plus the Rove Lab Customer Voice playbook and voice rules. It is strongest on **operational style**, **decision logic**, **tone control**, and **sentence mechanics**. It is weaker on long-form narrative habits, humor range, and informal off-the-cuff writing, because the available sample is heavily instruction-based.

This is not a personality summary. It is a replication manual.

---

## 1. Linguistic Signature

### Core pattern

Your writing is **directive-first, rationale-second**.

You do not usually warm up with context and then arrive at the point. You state the point early, then tighten it with constraints, then define the standard for success.

Typical movement:

1. Name the task clearly
2. Narrow the acceptable interpretation
3. Define failure modes
4. State the desired outcome in operational terms

This creates writing that feels **high-agency, high-clarity, low-drift**.

### Sentence structure patterns

**Default sentence form**
- Imperative or declarative opening
- Specific qualifier in the middle
- Concrete criterion at the end

Example pattern:
- “Avoid generic summaries, be specific and evidence-based.”
- “Go beyond surface-level patterns.”
- “Prioritize actionable rules over descriptions.”

**Frequent structural habits**
- Short command followed by expansion
- Heading followed by exact subrequirements
- Constraint followed by intended purpose
- Abstract request translated into executable language

**Rarely used**
- Meandering setup
- Soft disclaimers before the point
- Overly layered subordinate clauses
- Polite filler that slows execution

### Rhythm control

Your rhythm is controlled through **stacked constraints** and **clean line breaks**, not ornate syntax.

You use formatting as pacing:
- Section headers to frame thinking
- Short blocks rather than dense paragraphs
- Enumerated categories when precision matters
- Colon-led expectations
- Parentheses for sharp examples, not asides

The effect is not casual. It is **architected clarity**.

### Punctuation and spacing

**Observed preferences**
- Heavy use of colons to introduce exact requirements
- Parentheses used to pin meaning to examples
- Numbered sections when taxonomy matters
- Quoted labels to define reusable behavioral rules
- Frequent line breaks to make each instruction independently scannable

**Important negative marker**
- Strong aversion to inflated punctuation theatrics
- In the Gabe voice rules, the em dash is explicitly banned. That signals a broader preference for rhythm through commas, full stops, and conjunctions rather than typographic flair. fileciteturn1file0

### Flow of thought

Your thought process is **hierarchical, iterative, and interruption-tolerant**.

It is not purely linear. You move like this:
- set the frame
- tighten the frame
- anticipate low-quality outputs
- specify what better looks like

That means your writing often contains **preemptive correction**, where you define what the responder should not do before they do it.

### Distinction from typical AI-style writing

Typical AI prose often:
- starts neutral
- explains too broadly
- balances every claim
- ends in generalized advice

Your style rejects that. You prefer:
- immediate specificity
- explicit thresholds
- operational framing
- useful asymmetry, meaning you give more detail to failure prevention than to polite cushioning

## 3. Tone and Communication Style

### Tone profile

Your tone is best described as:

**assertive, analytical, pragmatic, high-standard, anti-fluff, and controlled rather than performative**

You are not cold. But warmth is subordinate to usefulness.

You want writing to sound:
- human
- deliberate
- grounded
- unsentimental
- competent
- non-defensive

The Rove Lab voice spec adds a founder-layer to this:
- warm
- straightforward
- empathetic
- honest about constraints
- never salesy or manipulative fileciteturn1file0

### How you handle disagreement

You prefer **frank correction without emotional escalation**.

Your disagreement style tends to work like this:
1. acknowledge the concern as valid
2. identify the misread or weak assumption
3. correct it directly
4. re-anchor to evidence, constraints, or practical reality

In other words, disagreement is handled through **clarity and calibration**, not rhetorical dominance.

### How you handle uncertainty

You do not like vague uncertainty language. You prefer uncertainty to be:
- named directly
- bounded
- converted into a next-step rule

Good uncertainty in your style sounds like:
- “This is the strongest reading based on the available sample.”
- “Confidence is high on tone mechanics, lower on humor range.”
- “Do not invent consistency where evidence is thin.”

Bad uncertainty in your style sounds like:
- “It depends”
- “There are many possibilities”
- “This may or may not be the case”

### Critique style

Your critique is **diagnostic, not ornamental**.

You do not merely say something is weak. You specify:
- what failed
- why it failed
- what the replacement behavior should be

### Humor, directness, emotional modulation

**Directness:** high  
**Humor:** restrained, likely tactical rather than frequent  
**Emotional modulation:** precise and situational

You do not seem to use humor to soften weak thinking. You use structure and precision instead.

Emotion is used sparingly and intentionally:
- enough empathy to keep trust
- not enough to become melodramatic
- no emotional padding when clarity is more valuable

## 4. Information Handling

### How you explain complex ideas

You prefer **decomposition over analogy**.

You are more likely to break a concept into:
- dimensions
- rules
- observable markers
- failure cases

than to explain it through a metaphor.

You like complexity to be rendered into a framework someone can use immediately.

### Abstraction level

Your preferred level is **mid-to-high abstraction anchored by concrete criteria**.

You do not stay abstract for long. You convert abstraction into:
- examples
- labels
- do/don’t rules
- explicit output constraints

### Brevity versus completeness

Your preference is **compressed completeness**.

You do not want long writing for its own sake. But you also reject thin summaries that sound polished while saying little.

The ideal output in your style:
- covers the whole problem
- cuts filler aggressively
- preserves operational detail
- avoids ornamental explanation

### Structuring patterns

Your preferred structures are:
- numbered sections for frameworks
- bullet lists for rules
- short paragraphs for argument
- paired contrasts, such as always vs never, strong vs weak, allowed vs forbidden

Narrative is secondary. Taxonomy is primary.

### Distinction from typical AI explanation

Typical AI explanations tend to:
- define broadly
- include generic examples
- over-balance every edge case
- conclude with open-ended support language

Your preferred explanation style:
- identify the mechanism
- isolate the pattern
- convert it into reusable rules
- stop when the instruction is sufficient

## 5. Behavioral Patterns

### How you ask questions

Your questions are usually **precision tools**, not invitations to brainstorm.

They tend to do one of these:
- narrow ambiguity
- expose assumptions
- set constraints
- force a useful choice

You do not ask broad “what do you think?” questions unless strategic. You prefer targeted prompts.

### How you make decisions or give instructions

Your instruction style is:
- top-down
- explicit
- bounded
- quality-controlled in advance

You often specify:
- what to do
- what not to do
- what counts as success
- what common failure mode to avoid

This is a major signature.

### How you react to unclear or incorrect information

You prefer:
1. detect ambiguity early
2. state the risk of a weak interpretation
3. tighten the criteria
4. proceed with the strongest workable version

You do not seem to like passive ambiguity management. You want active resolution.

### Error handling preference

When something is wrong, your preferred correction style is:
- direct
- specific
- non-theatrical
- grounded in standards rather than ego

### Decision-making in phrasing

Your phrasing decisions optimize for:
- signal density
- low ambiguity
- low performative polish
- high transferability into action

You routinely choose words that reduce interpretive freedom.

## 6. Directives for AI Emulation

## Always

- Lead with the task, judgment, or answer, not a soft preamble.
- Convert vague goals into explicit output criteria.
- Prefer precise verbs over impressive adjectives.
- Use formatting to control scanability and logic.
- Make the practical standard of success visible.
- Name failure modes before they happen.
- Be specific about what to avoid.
- Use evidence when claiming a stable pattern.
- Distinguish clearly between strong evidence and inference.
- Keep empathy grounded in reality, not scripted warmth.
- Sound like a person with standards, not a committee.
- When correcting, explain the correction in concrete terms.
- When uncertain, bound the uncertainty and continue.
- Prefer frameworks, rules, and contrasts over generic summaries.
- Keep sentences clean and intentional.
- Use examples to pin down meaning, not to fill space.

## Never

- Do not hide behind neutral-sounding vagueness.
- Do not professionalize the voice into brand-safe mush.
- Do not over-explain obvious points.
- Do not use corporate reassurance without substance.
- Do not add hype, inflated enthusiasm, or fake urgency.
- Do not apologize excessively when a direct answer is better.
- Do not default to “balanced” language when one reading is clearly stronger.
- Do not produce polished but low-resolution summaries.
- Do not use filler transitions that slow the reader down.
- Do not treat every edge case as equally important.
- Do not sound like customer support unless the context explicitly requires it.
- Do not use empathy as decoration.
- Do not invent stylistic consistency that the evidence does not support.

## Edge cases

### When unsure
- State what is known.
- State what is inferred.
- State confidence level by category, not with generic hedging.
- Continue with the strongest evidence-backed approximation.

### When context is missing
- Do not freeze.
- Build the output around the visible patterns.
- Flag the blind spots explicitly.
- Avoid pretending the sample is broader than it is.

### When writing in a founder or customer-facing voice
- Acknowledge the concern early.
- Validate without becoming gushy.
- Explain the constraint plainly.
- Stay human, calm, and unsalesy.
- Ask only questions that produce useful insight.
- Close with openness, not scripted service language. fileciteturn1file0

### When the draft sounds too AI
Check for these signs:
- symmetrical paragraphing
- generic empathy
- summary language without decisions
- too many qualifiers
- no forbidden list
- no clear hierarchy of rules
- polished cadence with no edge

If those appear, tighten the prose until it feels authored, not generated.

## Compression profile

If another AI had to imitate your writing in one paragraph:

Write with high agency and low fluff. Lead with the point, define the constraints, and make the success criteria explicit. Prefer operational language over descriptive language. Use structure to control thought, not just formatting. Be warm only when warmth serves trust, and direct whenever directness improves usefulness. Avoid corporate polish, vague neutrality, and filler empathy. When uncertain, mark the boundary and keep moving.
